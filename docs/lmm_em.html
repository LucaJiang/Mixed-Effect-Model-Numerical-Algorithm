Model Description

The relation between OLM and LMM can be found in The Introduction of Linear Mixed-Effect Model.

The linear mixed-effect model (LMM) is a statistical model that accounts for both fixed effects and random effects in a linear regression model. It is used for modeling data where observations are not independent or identically distributed.

Consider a dataset {y,X,Z}\{\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{X},\mathbf{Z}\}{y,X,Z} with nnn samples, where y∈Rn\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^ny∈Rn is the vector of response variable, X∈Rn×p\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}X∈Rn×p is the matrix of ppp independent variables, and Z∈Rn×c\mathbf{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times c}Z∈Rn×c is another matrix of ccc variables. The linear mixed model builds upon a linear relationship from y\mathbf{y}y to X\mathbf{X}X and Z\mathbf{Z}Z by
y=Zω⏟fixed+Xβ⏟random+e⏟error,\begin{equation} \mathbf{y} = \underbrace{\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega}}_{\text {fixed}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}}_{\text {random}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{e}}_{\text {error}}, \end{equation}y=fixedZω​​+randomXβ​​+errore​​,​​
where ω∈Rc\mathbf{\omega} \in \mathbb{R}^cω∈Rc is the vector of fixed effects, β∈Rp\mathbf{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^pβ∈Rp is the vector of random effects with β∼N(0,σβ2Ip)\mathbf{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2_\mathbf{\beta} \mathbf{I}_p)β∼N(0,σβ2​Ip​), and e∼N(0,σe2In)\mathbf{e} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2_e \mathbf{I}_n)e∼N(0,σe2​In​) is the independent noise term.

Let Θ\mathbf{\Theta}Θ denote the set of unknown parameters Θ={ω,σβ2,σe2}\mathbf{\Theta} = \{\mathbf{\omega}, \sigma^2_\mathbf{\beta}, \sigma^2_e\}Θ={ω,σβ2​,σe2​}. Under the framework of EM algorithm, we can treat β\mathbf{\beta}β as a latent variable. Below is the directed acyclic graph below for our model.

In the following sections, we will first find the posterior distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β given y\mathbf{y}y and Θ^\hat{\mathbf{\Theta}}Θ^ in the E-step. Then we will assume that β\mathbf{\beta}β has been estimated by β^\hat{\mathbf{\beta}}β^​ and find the ML estimator of Θ\mathbf{\Theta}Θ which is used in M-step. The EM algorithm is an iterative algorithm that alternates between the E-step and the M-step until convergence. Finally, we will calculate and track the complete data log-likelihood ℓc(Θ)\ell_c(\mathbf{\Theta})ℓc​(Θ) to check the convergence of the algorithm.

Statistical Inference in LMM

Question: Can we use y−Zω∣Θ,β∼N(Xβ,σβ2XXT+σe2In)\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega}| \mathbf{\Theta}, \mathbf{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}, \sigma_\beta^2\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^T + \sigma_e^2\mathbf{I}_n)y−Zω∣Θ,β∼N(Xβ,σβ2​XXT+σe2​In​)?[1] The following derivation are based on Ref 1.

Statistical Inference in E-Step

The E-step aims to find the posterior distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β given y\mathbf{y}y and Θ\mathbf{\Theta}Θ. The complete data log-likelihood is given by
ℓc(Θ)=log⁡p(y,β,Θ)=log⁡p(β∣y,Θ)+log⁡p(y∣Θ)\begin{equation} \begin{split} \ell_c(\mathbf{\Theta}) &= \log p(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta})\\ &= \log p(\mathbf{\beta}| \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta}) + \log p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\Theta}) \end{split}\end{equation}ℓc​(Θ)​=logp(y,β,Θ)=logp(β∣y,Θ)+logp(y∣Θ)​​​
where p(y∣Θ)p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\Theta})p(y∣Θ) dose not depend on β\mathbf{\beta}β. However the posterior distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β can not be obtained directly. Instead, by applying Bayes' rule, we have
p(β∣y,Θ)=p(y∣β,Θ)p(β∣Θ)p(y∣Θ)∝p(y∣β,Θ)p(β∣Θ)∝exp⁡{−12(y−Zω−Xβ)T(σe2In)−1(y−Zω−Xβ)}×exp⁡{−12βT(σβ2Ip)−1β}∝exp⁡{−12(β−μ)TΓ−1(β−μ)}\begin{equation} \begin{split} p(\mathbf{\beta}| \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta}) &= \frac{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta}) p(\mathbf{\beta}| \mathbf{\Theta})}{p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\Theta})}\\ &\propto p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta}) p(\mathbf{\beta}| \mathbf{\Theta})\\ &\propto \exp \left\{-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta})^T (\sigma_e^2\mathbf{I}_n)^{-1} (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}) \right\} \\ &\quad \times \exp \left\{-\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{\beta}^T (\sigma_\mathbf{\beta}^2 \mathbf{I}_p)^{-1} \mathbf{\beta} \right\} \\ &\propto \exp \left\{-\frac{1}{2}\left( \mathbf{\beta} - \mathbf{\mu}\right)^T \Gamma^{-1} \left( \mathbf{\beta} - \mathbf{\mu}\right) \right\} \end{split}\end{equation}p(β∣y,Θ)​=p(y∣Θ)p(y∣β,Θ)p(β∣Θ)​∝p(y∣β,Θ)p(β∣Θ)∝exp{−21​(y−Zω−Xβ)T(σe2​In​)−1(y−Zω−Xβ)}×exp{−21​βT(σβ2​Ip​)−1β}∝exp{−21​(β−μ)TΓ−1(β−μ)}​​​

where Γ=(XTXσe2+Ipσβ2)−1\Gamma = \left(\frac{\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}}{\sigma_e^2} + \frac{\mathbf{I}_p}{\sigma_\mathbf{\beta}^2} \right)^{-1}Γ=(σe2​XTX​+σβ2​Ip​​)−1 and μ=ΓXT(y−Zω)/σe2\mathbf{\mu} = \Gamma \mathbf{X}^T (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega})/\sigma_e^2μ=ΓXT(y−Zω)/σe2​. And, the posterior distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β is β∣y,Θ∼N(μ,Γ)\mathbf{\beta}|\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{\mu}, \Gamma)β∣y,Θ∼N(μ,Γ).

Statistical Inference in M-Step

If β\mathbf{\beta}β has been estimated, the conditional distribution of y\mathbf{y}y become
y∣Θ,β∼N(Zω+Xβ,σe2In)\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\Theta}, \mathbf{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega}+ \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}, \sigma_e^2\mathbf{I}_n)y∣Θ,β∼N(Zω+Xβ,σe2​In​)

Then the ML estimator of Θ\mathbf{\Theta}Θ maximizes the complete data log-likelihood:
ℓc(Θ)=log⁡p(y,β,Θ)=log⁡p(y∣β,Θ)+log⁡p(β∣Θ)\begin{equation} \begin{split} \ell_c(\Theta) &= \log p(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta})\\ &= \log p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta}) + \log p(\mathbf{\beta}| \Theta)\end{split}\end{equation}ℓc​(Θ)​=logp(y,β,Θ)=logp(y∣β,Θ)+logp(β∣Θ)​​​

The marginal log-likelihood is given by
log⁡p(y∣β,Θ)=−n2log⁡(2π)−12log⁡∣σe2In∣−12(y−Zω−Xβ)T(σe2In)−1(y−Zω−Xβ)=−n2log⁡(2π)−n2log⁡σe2−12σe2∥y−Zω−Xβ∥2\begin{equation} \begin{split} \log p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta}) &= -\frac{n}{2} \log (2\pi) -\frac{1}{2} \log |\sigma_e^2\mathbf{I}_n| \\&\quad - \frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta})^T (\sigma_e^2\mathbf{I}_n)^{-1} (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta})\\ &= -\frac{n}{2} \log (2\pi) -\frac{n}{2} \log \sigma_e^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_e^2} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}\|^2 \end{split}\end{equation}logp(y∣β,Θ)​=−2n​log(2π)−21​log∣σe2​In​∣−21​(y−Zω−Xβ)T(σe2​In​)−1(y−Zω−Xβ)=−2n​log(2π)−2n​logσe2​−2σe2​1​∥y−Zω−Xβ∥2​​​
and
log⁡p(β∣Θ)=−p2log⁡(2π)−p2log⁡σβ2−12σβ2βTβ\begin{equation} \begin{split} \log p(\mathbf{\beta}| \Theta) &= -\frac{p}{2} \log (2\pi) -\frac{p}{2} \log \sigma_\beta^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_\beta^2} \mathbf{\beta}^T \mathbf{\beta} \end{split}\end{equation}logp(β∣Θ)​=−2p​log(2π)−2p​logσβ2​−2σβ2​1​βTβ​​​

Since, the posterior distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β is β∣y,Θ∼N(μ,Γ)\mathbf{\beta}|\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{\mu}, \Gamma)β∣y,Θ∼N(μ,Γ).

Complete Data Log-Likelihood

The complete data log-likelihood is given by
ℓc(Θ)=log⁡p(y,β,Θ)=−n2log⁡(2π)−n2log⁡σe2−12σe2∥y−Zω−Xβ∥2−p2log⁡(2π)−p2log⁡σβ2−12σβ2βTβ\begin{equation} \begin{split} \ell_c(\mathbf{\Theta}) &= \log p(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta})\\ &= -\frac{n}{2} \log (2\pi) -\frac{n}{2} \log \sigma_e^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_e^2} \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}\|^2\\ &\quad -\frac{p}{2} \log (2\pi) -\frac{p}{2} \log \sigma_\beta^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_\beta^2} \mathbf{\beta}^T \mathbf{\beta} \end{split}\end{equation}ℓc​(Θ)​=logp(y,β,Θ)=−2n​log(2π)−2n​logσe2​−2σe2​1​∥y−Zω−Xβ∥2−2p​log(2π)−2p​logσβ2​−2σβ2​1​βTβ​​​

EM Algorithm

E-Step

The E-step is to compute the expectation of the complete data log-likelihood with respect to the conditional distribution of β\mathbf{\beta}β given y\mathbf{y}y and Θ(t)\Theta^{(t)}Θ(t):
Q(Θ∣Θ(t))=Eβ∣y,Θ(t)[log⁡p(y,β∣Θ)]=Eβ∣y,Θ(t)[log⁡p(y∣β,Θ)+log⁡p(β∣Θ)]\begin{equation} \begin{split} Q(\Theta| \mathbf{\Theta}^{(t)}) &= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{\beta}|\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta}^{(t)}} \left[ \log p(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\beta}| \Theta) \right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{\beta}|\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{\Theta}^{(t)}} \left[ \log p(\mathbf{y}| \mathbf{\beta}, \mathbf{\Theta}) + \log p(\mathbf{\beta}| \Theta) \right] \end{split}\end{equation}Q(Θ∣Θ(t))​=Eβ∣y,Θ(t)​[logp(y,β∣Θ)]=Eβ∣y,Θ(t)​[logp(y∣β,Θ)+logp(β∣Θ)]​​​

Thus, the E-step is to compute the following expectations:

β^(t+1)=μ(t)\begin{equation} \hat{\mathbf{\beta}}^{(t+1)}= \mathbf{\mu}^{(t)} \end{equation}β^​(t+1)=μ(t)​​

where Γ=(XTXσe2+Ipσβ2)−1\Gamma = \left(\frac{\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}}{\sigma_e^2} + \frac{\mathbf{I}_p}{\sigma_\mathbf{\beta}^2} \right)^{-1}Γ=(σe2​XTX​+σβ2​Ip​​)−1 and μ=ΓXT(y−Zω)/σe2\mathbf{\mu} = \Gamma \mathbf{X}^T (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega})/\sigma_e^2μ=ΓXT(y−Zω)/σe2​.

The M-step is to maximize the expectation of the complete data log-likelihood with respect to Θ\mathbf{\Theta}Θ:
Θ(t+1)=arg⁡max⁡ΘQ(Θ∣Θ(t))\begin{equation} \mathbf{\Theta}^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_\mathbf{\Theta} Q(\mathbf{\Theta}|\mathbf{\Theta}^{(t)}) \end{equation}Θ(t+1)=argΘmax​Q(Θ∣Θ(t))​​
which is equivalent to maximizing (2) and according to the results in (8), we have
ω^(t+1)=(ZTZ)−1ZT(y−Xμ(t+1))σ^β2(t+1)=1p(tr(Γ(t+1))+∥μ(t+1)∥2)σ^e2(t+1)=1n(∥y−Zω(t+1)∥2+tr(Γ(t+1)XTX)+∥Xμ(t+1)∥2−2(y−Zω(t+1))TXμ(t+1))\begin{equation} \begin{split} \hat{\mathbf{\omega}}^{(t+1)} &= (\mathbf{Z}^T \mathbf{Z})^{-1} \mathbf{Z}^T (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\mu}^{(t+1)})\\ \hat{\mathbf{\sigma}}_\beta^{2(t+1)} &= \frac{1}{p} \left(\text{tr}(\Gamma^{(t+1)}) + \| \mathbf{\mu}^{(t+1)}\|^2 \right)\\ \hat{\mathbf{\sigma}}_e^{2(t+1)} &= \frac{1}{n}\left( \|\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega}^{(t+1)}\|^2 + \text{tr}\left(\Gamma^{(t+1)}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X}\right)+ \|\mathbf{X}\mathbf{\mu}^{(t+1)} \|^2 - 2(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{\omega}^{(t+1)})^T \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\mu}^{(t+1)}\right) \end{split}\end{equation}ω^(t+1)σ^β2(t+1)​σ^e2(t+1)​​=(ZTZ)−1ZT(y−Xμ(t+1))=p1​(tr(Γ(t+1))+∥μ(t+1)∥2)=n1​(∥y−Zω(t+1)∥2+tr(Γ(t+1)XTX)+∥Xμ(t+1)∥2−2(y−Zω(t+1))TXμ(t+1))​​​
Then the complete data log-likelihood is given by

The Pseudocode of EM Algorithm

The EM algorithm is an iterative algorithm that alternates between the E-step and the M-step until convergence. The algorithm is summarized as follows:

  1. Initialize Θ(0)\mathbf{\Theta}^{(0)}Θ(0) and β\mathbf{\beta}β randomly.
  2. For t=0,1,…t = 0, 1, \dotst=0,1,…, MAX_ITERATION:
    1. E-step: Estimate β^(t)\hat{\mathbf{\beta}}^{(t)}β^​(t).
    2. M-step: Estimate Θ^(t+1)={ω^(t+1),σ^β2(t+1),σ^e2(t+1)}\hat{\mathbf{\Theta}}^{(t+1)}=\{\hat{\mathbf{\omega}}^{(t+1)}, \hat{\sigma}_\beta^{2(t+1)}, \hat{\sigma}_e^{2(t+1)}\}Θ^(t+1)={ω^(t+1),σ^β2(t+1)​,σ^e2(t+1)​}.
    3. Check ∣Δℓc∣|\Delta \ell_c|∣Δℓc​∣ for convergence. If converged, stop. Otherwise, continue.
  3. Return results.
